Published in 1986, *[It](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=It+Stephen+King&tag=YOURTAG-20)* is Stephen King's longest and most structurally ambitious novel — over 1,100 pages telling one story across two timelines simultaneously. It's also one of his most personal: a book about how childhood trauma doesn't go away, it just waits.

## The setup

In the town of Derry, Maine, children keep disappearing and dying, and have been for as long as anyone can remember, in clusters roughly 27 years apart. Seven kids — Bill, Ben, Beverly, Richie, Eddie, Mike, and Stan, who come to call themselves the Losers' Club — discover that the deaths are caused by an ancient, shape-shifting entity that mostly appears as a clown named Pennywise, and that feeds on fear as much as on the children themselves. In the summer of 1958, the Losers track It to the sewers beneath Derry and nearly kill it, then make a childhood pact: if It ever comes back, they'll come back too.

## The two timelines

The novel alternates between 1958, when the Losers are children first confronting It, and 1985, when they're adults — having scattered across the country and largely forgotten their childhoods, a side effect of leaving Derry — and are called back after the deaths start again. Each chapter shifts between an adult Loser's present-day investigation and the memory it triggers of what happened to them as kids. That structure is the whole engine of the book: it's not just telling two stories, it's showing how the adult characters are, piece by piece, forced to remember what they buried.

## What happens at the end

The adult Losers descend into the sewers beneath Derry and confront It in its true lair. King reveals that Pennywise is only a mask — It's actual nature is something closer to a cosmic, ancient force from outside ordinary reality, and the closest its true form can be rendered to the human mind is a giant spider-like creature. The Losers perform a ritual (the "Ritual of Chüd") that requires facing It with belief and will rather than physical weapons, since fear is what feeds it. Eddie is killed defending the others. The rest manage to tear out It's heart, ending it for good — and discover, grimly, that It was about to lay eggs, implying the threat could have kept renewing itself if they hadn't finished it.

Afterward, the surviving Losers immediately start forgetting again, the same way they did as children — Derry's memory-erasing effect on anyone who leaves. By the final pages, most of them can barely recall the details of what just happened, only that they loved each other and that it mattered. That fading-memory ending is deliberate: King is underlining that the point was never really the monster, it's what the characters did for each other.

## What the book is actually about

Underneath the monster, *It* is a book about growing up — specifically, about how the fears and traumas of childhood (abusive parents, bullying, poverty, racism, being different) don't resolve on their own, they get buried, and they resurface unless they're actually confronted. Derry itself is written as complicit: the adults in town are consistently unable or unwilling to see what's happening to their children, a pattern King uses to suggest that willful adult blindness is its own kind of monster, one that doesn't need anything supernatural to keep hurting kids.

The other major theme is the power of belief and unity. It feeds on fear and is genuinely weakened by the Losers' friendship and collective will — the book's argument, made pretty directly, is that isolation is what makes people vulnerable and that facing terrible things together is what actually defeats them. It's why the "forgetting" ending isn't a downer so much as the point: the friendship did its job even after the memory of it fades.

## The adaptations, if you're coming from the movies

The 1990 TV miniseries and the 2017/2019 two-part film series (*It* and *It: Chapter Two*) both adapt the novel but simplify or change its structure and ending significantly — notably, both films drop the book's more cosmic explanation of what It actually is, along with several of the book's more controversial elements. HBO's prequel series *Welcome to Derry*, set decades before the Losers' Club timeline, expands on Derry's history of violence and It's earlier appearances, adding material that isn't in the novel at all. If you've only seen the adaptations, the novel is a genuinely different, stranger experience — read it for the version of the story that isn't trying to fit into two movies.

## Should you read it?

If you can commit to the length, yes — *It* is frequently cited as one of King's best books for a reason, and it's the fullest demonstration of what he does across his career: horror that's actually about something.
